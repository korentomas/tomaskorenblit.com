In the age of AI, the most valuable data isn't necessarily the most abundant—it's the most connected. While companies race to accumulate user data, the real competitive moat lies in understanding the *relationships* between data points.

## The Network Capital Problem

Traditional CRMs treat relationships as isolated records. You have a contact, maybe some notes, a few interactions logged. But real-world networks don't work that way. Your network is a living, breathing graph where:

- Second-degree connections often matter more than first-degree
- Relationship strength decays over time
- Introduction paths have measurable value
- Network effects compound exponentially

## Why Graphs?

Graph databases and algorithms let us model reality more accurately. Instead of forcing relationship data into tables, we represent it as nodes and edges—just like the real world.

```python
# Traditional approach: flat data
contacts = [
  {"name": "Alice", "company": "TechCorp"},
  {"name": "Bob", "company": "StartupXYZ"}
]

# Graph approach: connected data
graph = {
  "Alice": {"works_at": "TechCorp", "knows": ["Bob", "Charlie"]},
  "Bob": {"works_at": "StartupXYZ", "introduced_by": "Alice"}
}
```

The graph approach immediately surfaces opportunities: Alice can introduce you to Bob. Bob works at a company you're targeting. Charlie is a second-degree connection worth warming up.

## The AI Layer

Layering ML on top of graph data unlocks entirely new capabilities:

1. **Relationship Scoring**: Predict which connections are most valuable
2. **Introduction Routing**: Find optimal paths between people
3. **Network Health**: Identify weak links before they break
4. **Opportunity Detection**: Surface high-value connections automatically
